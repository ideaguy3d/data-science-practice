2025 Senior Systems Analyst — Practical Examination Response

1) You are given an existing program with full source code and access to its data sources. How would you approach supporting and planning for the future of this program?
I bring the app up in a safe environment, readme/commits first, then draw a quick data/flow diagram. I list bugs, risks, and performance hot spots. Next, I stabilize: add basic tests, pin dependencies, remove hard-coded secrets, set up simple CI and a rollback. I meet stakeholders to confirm what must not break, what can wait, and what to improve. I publish a small roadmap (stability → reliability → new value) with clear owners, SLAs, and success metrics. I finish with plain runbooks and “how to build/deploy/restore” docs.
2) Describe how you would lead a project where you’re tasked with providing a technical solution to a client’s business problem.
I start with a short discovery: problem, users, constraints, risks, and success measures. I translate this into a simple charter and a prioritized backlog. I run short iterations with demos so the client sees progress early. I de-risk with a quick spike on the hardest part. I keep security, data, and infra in scope from day one. Before go-live: UAT, cutover plan, and support plan. After go-live: training, docs, and a brief “what we learned.”

3) Suppose you need to pull data from one source and integrate it into another. How would you approach this problem considering multiple formats (flat files, APIs, point-to-point connections)?
I write a short mapping: keys, fields, types, and cadence. Data lands in a staging area, then transforms, then merges into target (idempotent). Flat files: schema checks, safe batching, clear rejects. APIs: auth, paging, retries, backoff, and audit logs. Point-to-point: prefer a queue/middleware to decouple. I add simple data-quality rules (nulls, ranges, dupes), alerts, and a replay path. A lightweight dashboard shows latency, volume, and failures.
4) You’re working in a team where several strong personalities insist on an approach that you believe is not the best solution. How would you handle this situation?
I make options visible side-by-side with agreed criteria: reliability, security, cost, time, and support load. We run a short, time-boxed prototype to gather data. I separate people from ideas, note trade-offs, and look for “good now, better later.” If needed, I bring in a neutral reviewer and document the decision with a check-in date tied to metrics.
5) How do you adjust your communication style when discussing complex technical topics with technical peers versus non-technical stakeholders?
With peers: precise terms, diagrams, benchmarks, and trade-offs. With non-technical folks: plain language, outcomes, timelines, risks, and cost; one simple visual per idea. In both cases I confirm understanding and next steps in writing.
